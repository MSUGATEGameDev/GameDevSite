Distributing Your Game

Find out where to put your game for others to see it

PC Distribution

Steam is probably the most popular storefront, with a lot of developer analytics tools, DRM, and lot of visibility but a lot of people competing for the spotlight.
Getting your game on Steam also gives it an easy route to their non-windows platforms like SteamOS.
Steam will have staff review your game to ensure it meets basic quality and security standards. You will likely have to go back and forth a bit while you fix bugs they found, but eventually if you comply with a low set of standards, you will get in.
Steam takes 30% of revenue made on its store and requires a $100 entry fee per-game to list a game (which it returns when the game has made $1,000).

Epic is probably the next biggest PC platform.
Epic will also have staff review your game for basic functionality and security, but you will eventually get in if you meet basic standards.
Epic also charges $100 per game, but only takes a 12% cut of the revenue.

itch.io is a very popular platform for independent developers online because it has a much lower barrier to entry.
There is no team reviewing the apps that go on the store, so your game doesn't even need to work to be posted.
There is no listing fee and the percentage cut to the store is entirely optional, so you can post completely free.

There are many other stores that may suit a niche you fall into. Before you post a game on a store, make sure to verify all the terms, ensuring money distribution and IP ownership is clear.
There is also nothing stopping you from hosting your own store and letting people download on your site. It doesn't quite get the visibility of a major store though.

Mobile Distribution

Google Play is pretty much the only store if you want to sell your game on Android.
They have a $25 one-time fee to sign up as a developer, and then they take 30% of your revenue from Android game sales.

Amazon's Kindle Fire platform runs on Android too, so you can upload your Android games to Kindle as well.
They have no sign-up fees, just 30% of your revenue.
Being a smaller market, there tends to be less competition than there is on Google Play.

Apple's iOS platform requires a $100 annual fee to remain on their store, as well as 30% of your revenue.
