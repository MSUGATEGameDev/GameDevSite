Definitions

Explaining some of the unique words and products in the world of game development

3D Model

The visual representation of an object, usable in gaming or animation. This is different than the actual object that exists once you place the model into the game.

Amazon App Store

An online storefront that sells Android games for Kindle Fire devices.
Developers do not pay any initial fees to sign up, but pay Amazon 30% of their revenue from transactions made through the store.

Android

A power-efficient open-source operating system that is used on many mobile phones, tablets, e-readers, watches, smart TVs, car media systems, and smart appliances.

Asset

The catch-all term for all the components that go into making a video game. Unity has an asset folder that contains your code, models, prefabs, and plugins.

Axis

(Pronounced axe-iss)(Plural: Axes, Pronounced axe-eez)
A direction in 3D space. We generally refer to objects in game as existing at a point along (and a rotation around) 3 axes: X,Y,Z.
Unity, keeping 2D games in mind, uses X and Y for horizontal and Vertical, and Z for depth (or the other horizontal axis in 3D games).

Blender

A free open-source 3D design program that allows you to created 3D models to be used in animation or video games. It also has an animator for making 3D animated movies.

C++

(C Plus Plus) is a variant of an older coding language called C. It allows a high degree of control over the efficiency of a program, but is more complicated to work with.

C#

(C Sharp) is a variant of an older coding language called C. It is easy to work with but hands off a lot of the efficiency control to the computer.

Coding

The process of writing machine-readable instructions, generally in plain text. This is the foundation of how all programs and games are built.

DevKit

A piece of hardware similar to (but not usually exactly the same as) a video game console. These are provided to, loaned to, or purchased by devekopers to build and test their games, to ensure they will function properly on the standardized hardware.
Some platforms (like Meta Quest) simply allow testing on commercially-avaialble hardware, while some platforms (like PC) are not standardized enough to guarantee all iterations and do not have devkits.

Download Rights Management

A method of controlling who is able to download,install, or run a program on their device. Since digital media can be copied indefinitely, DRM prevents a malicious storefront from purchasing one copy of a game and redistributing it indefinitely for a lower price. It would also prevent a friend from making a copy of a game for another friend, requiring both friends to purchase a copy. This is generally performed by the developer of a game working with a storefront to require a launcher login before playing a game.

It does not necessarily equate one-to-one. Some stores allow a degree of sharing: Steam allows 6 friends/family to form a group and share their games with each other. Google Play allows you to share apps with your 6-person Google family. Meta Quest allows you to play your games on any headset while sharing your games on one headset. There are similar programs on some other platforms.

Epic Games

Epic Games is the owner of the Epic Games Store, a major digital PC game storefront.
The Epic Games Store is a generally open platform that will allow any developer, provided they comply with several basic standards. Games are individually reviewed by Epic employees. Each game submission costs $100 and epic takes 12% of all revenue made on the platform.

Epic games is also the creator of Unreal Engine and the popular game Fortnite.

Game Engine

A program that helps you combine all the elements of a video game into a cohesive product. It handles the complexity of interfacing with the operating system, monitors, speakers, input devices; and your 3D models, code, sounds, and pictures; providing you with simple processes for connecting them all together.

GATE

Michigan State University Gifted and Talented Education is the program this class was prepared for. It is the university's outreach program for gifted and talended primary and secondary students.

GitHub

GitHub is a popular backup and collaboration website built off of an open-source version control and backup program called Git.
It is what we will be using in this class and highly recommend you use personally for tracking changes to your project, collaborating with others, and backing up in case of disaster.

Godot

(Commonly pronounced G-doh) is an full-featured, fully-free, open-source game engine. It is not as robust or complete as Unity or Unreal Engine, but it has great community support and is growing quickly.

Google Play Store

The main store for Android Apps on mobile phones running Android.
Google Play has a $25 one-time fee to register as a developer and takes %30 of revenue made in the store. App submissions have to pass some automated tests, but are not generally reviewed by a human.

Hypertext Markup Language

The primary coding language used by websites. It is a form of XML, which is essentially standard text, manipulated by commands wrapped in angle brackets.

Independent Developer

(Or Indie Dev) A game design studio who is not backed by a Developer. Developers will often provide the money a studio needs to pay for equipment, employees, and other resources with a contract to take a (usually large) portion of the profits when the game is completed. Independent studios get no such support while developing the game, but then generally receive most of the profits from the game.

Integrated Development Environment

An advanced text editor made to help you write code.
It may have color coding, auto formatting, error detection, and auto-complete features. It may also have functions to let you compile, run, and test your code as you're making it.

iOS App Store

Apple's storefront for selling iOS games to run on iPhones and iPads.
Developers pay $100 a year to be able to use Apple's developer tools and keep their apps on the store, as well as paying Apple 30% of revenue from purchases made on the store. Apps submitted to the store are reivewed by automated checks as well as human reviewers.

IP

In our context, IP generally refers to Intellectual Property. It is the design, symbols, names, images, and story that turn a work of art into a legally defensible property.
IP can also refer to Internet Protocol, the method used for locating another computer on the internet with a binary code known as an IP Address.

itch.io

A popular digital storefront for PC games, assets, and games on other platforms that can be installed from PC.
They make their hosting fees entirely optional and developer-defined, and do not review content posted to the platform, making it an ideal location for indie developers to launch a beginner/experimental game.

Kindle Fire

The Android-based platform used for Amazon's full-color Kindle devices. It is generally compatible with most applications that run on other Android devices, but is locked inside of its own storefront, the Amazon App Store.

Linux

A free open-source operating system often used for servers and smart devices, and increasingly more for personal computing as more and more people are frustrated with decisions made for Windows and MacOS platforms. SteamOS and Android are built upon Linux.

Long-Term Support

Game Engines like to add new features, fix bugs from previous versions, and make changes in general. As a developer, it can be tiring to keep up with the changes and ensure nothing created a new bug with your game that you weren't aware of. Long-term support (LTS) versions promise to avoid changes for a long period of time and provide security/bug fixes and customer support where needed.

Meta Quest

(Formerly Oculus) The Virtual Reality product from Meta (formerly Facebook). While Meta does have some PCVR presence, it has largely abandoned PCVR for its Android-based mobile headset the Meta Quest.

The Meta Quest is an entirely standalone device that can run VR games entirely on its own, instead of a PC or game console.

The Meta Quest Store does not have any initial fees to sign up or publish a game and it takes 30% of revenue made on its store. Each app submission is reviewed by a Meta employee for a basic level of compliance with performance and safety, but the store is not restricted.

Non-Disclosure Agreement

A legal document outlining the boundaries of secrets related to a company or project and penaties to be inflicted upon the assignee if they leak said secrets.
Due to the competitive nature of the games industry, many console manufacturers, developers, and publishers require an NDA to work with them.

Nintendo

The company behind the Nintendo Switch (including a long line of previous consoles of course).
Nintendo's platform is restricted. Developers must provide Nintendo with a portfolio of past projects and an idea for the game they would like to develop for (or port to) Switch. Approved developers must sign an NDA and pay somewhere between $500 and $1000 for a devkit.

Open-Source

A program, game, project, where the code is publicly avaialble to be read, scrutinized by everyone, allowing anyone to recommend changes, use, or build their own versions of the project. Open-source projects are still generally controlled by an individual or organization to maintain the integrity of a project from well-meaning but ignorant (or full-on malicious) contributors.

Origin

The point in a 3D model that corresponds to the position given the model by the system it's used in. Oftentimes this may be middle, but it may be more convenient for the origin to be horizontal middle, but at the vertical lowest point, to simplify the computation needed to place it on a surface.

PlayStation

The gaming platform from Sony. Playstation's store is restricted. Developers must provide a portfolio of past projects and an idea for the game they would like to develop for (or port to) Playstation. Approved developers sign an NDA, likely pay a decent sum of money to pay for expenses, and receive a devkit.

Plugin

A series of scripts and game objects that was created by someone else and made available for others to download and "plug in" to their game, saving newer developers from additional unneeded efforts.

Prefab

When you combine multiple game assets into a functioning game object that does something specific (such as a model, combined with a script to make a car), that object can be made into a prefab, which allows you to copy it and use it anywhere without building it from scratch.

Repository

(or Repo) A digital storage folder (generally located in the cloud) where you keep all the files, folders, code, images, and documentation related to a specific project. Unlike a regular computer folder, a repository tracks and preserves the entire history of every change made to your files, allowing you to seamlessly revert to previous versions if needed.

Steam

The largest digital PC games store. Owned by Valve, the creators of a string of highly succesfful games in the early 2000s.
Steam takes 30% of revenue made on its store and requires a $100 entry fee per-game to list a game (which it returns when the game has made $1,000).
Steam's store is not restricted. Any developer can sell games there. Steam will have staff review your game to ensure it meets basic quality and security standards.

Steam OS

A linux-based operating system built by Valve, the creators of Steam which removes most of the complexity of the OS to get players directly to the games in their Steam Library. It is avaiable for people to install on their own hardware, but it is most know as the operating system on Valve's dedicated hardware like the SteamDeck and SteamMachine.

Syntax

The rules of a coding language, telling you what part of your text the computer will be able to interpret properly.

Unity

The Game Engine we use in our class, that is, in our opinion, the most beginner-friendly of the full-featured publicly-available engines.
It is completely free for developers who make less than $200,000 annually from games made in Unity, and costs $2,000 a year for those who make over $200,000 from Unity games in that year (at most, 1% of revenue).

Unreal Engine

The Game Engine widely considered to be the most powerful publicly available engine. It is capable of creating very detailed games, but is rather difficult for beginners.
It is completely free for developers who have made less than $1 million from games made with Unreal, and they charge a 5% royalty on all revenue made after $1 million.

Version Control

The ability to save your project at various stages of completion, and go back and revert to a previous version if the most recent updates have caused unfavorable output. This is one of the most basic services provided by Git and GitHub.

Visual Studio

An IDE with color coding, auto formatting, error detection, and auto-complete features which make it easier to write code for Unity. It comes bundled with Unity as a free add-on.

Xbox

The gaming platform from Microsoft. Xbox's store is restricted. Developers must provide a portfolio of past projects and an idea for the game they would like to develop for (or port to) Xbox. Approved developers sign an NDA, likely pay a decent sum of money to pay for expenses, and receive a devkit.
